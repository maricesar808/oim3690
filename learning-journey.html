From my first webpage to work that feels like mine.

Over ten weeks, I learned HTML, CSS, JavaScript, APIs, and how to work with AI. The biggest change was not just what I could build. It was learning when to keep an AI suggestion, when to change it, and when to start over.

Ten weeks, five chapters

The arc

I started with the basics, built for real people, struggled through JavaScript, connected live data, and finished by making stronger choices about scope and design.

Weeks 1–2

Learning how a webpage works

I set up GitHub and VS Code, built my first personal website, and learned semantic HTML, heading order, images, links, and alt text. At first, I kept most of what AI generated and changed the colors and wording to fit me.

HTMLGitHubAccessibility
Weeks 3–4

Building for someone I care about

For my first mini project, I interviewed my grandma and turned her handwritten recipes into a multi-page family cookbook. This taught me that a website should begin with the client's needs, not just my own design preferences.

CSSClient interviewResponsive design
Weeks 5–6

My hardest stretch: JavaScript

Functions, inputs, conditions, and DOM selectors were new and confusing. I built a to-do app, meal decision helper, converter, and discount calculator. Instead of only accepting finished code, I started asking AI to explain each part so I could read it myself.

JavaScriptFunctionsIf / else
Weeks 7–8

Working with live data—and checking it

My Beach Day Tracker used weather data to rate conditions at Oahu beaches. When AI gave me inaccurate coordinates, I caught the problem, researched the correct locations, and fixed them. That was when I really understood that confident AI answers still need to be verified.

APIsfetch()Fact-checking
Weeks 9–10

Making deliberate choices

For my final project, I built a professional website for my tennis coach, Reyn Miyagawa. I focused on useful contact forms, and rejected an AI redesign that did not feel right. I used Aritzia's clean aesthetic as a clearer reference and shaped the final look around my own goal.

ScopeDesign directionDeployment
“AI can give me a starting point, but I am responsible for the final result.”

The biggest change

In the beginning, I judged AI output mostly by whether it worked. By the end, I was also asking whether it was accurate, useful, personal, and right for the person I was building for.
